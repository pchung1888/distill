The Water Cycle: How Earth Recycles Its Water

The water cycle, also called the hydrologic cycle, describes the continuous
movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. The cycle
has no starting point, but solar energy drives the water cycle: the sun
heats water in oceans, lakes, and rivers, causing some of it to evaporate
into the atmosphere as water vapor. Plants also release water vapor into
the atmosphere through a process called transpiration, in which water drawn
up from the soil escapes through small pores in their leaves.

As moist air rises, it cools, and the water vapor condenses into clouds
made of tiny droplets or ice crystals. When these droplets grow heavy
enough, they fall back to the surface as precipitation, such as rain or
snow. In cold regions, precipitation can accumulate as snowpack and
glaciers, storing water in frozen form for months or even centuries.

Once precipitation reaches the ground, it follows one of several paths.
Some of it soaks into the soil in a process called infiltration, which
recharges groundwater aquifers -- underground layers of porous rock that
hold fresh water. The rest becomes surface runoff, flowing downhill into
streams and rivers that eventually return the water to the oceans, where
the cycle begins again.

The oceans dominate Earth's water budget: about 97 percent of Earth's
water is stored in the oceans as salt water. Of the small fraction that is
fresh water, most is locked in ice caps and glaciers, leaving less than one
percent of all water readily available in lakes, rivers, and shallow
groundwater. Because the total amount of water on Earth is essentially
fixed, the water cycle is a true recycling system: the same water molecules
have been evaporating, condensing, and falling as rain for billions of
years.
