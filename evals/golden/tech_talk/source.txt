[01:10] Two years ago we had one PostgreSQL primary holding about 12
terabytes of data, and our p99 query latency on the events table was

migration was a white-knuckle event.

[03:45] The first fix was the boring one: connection pooling. Our API
servers were opening thousands of short-lived connections, and Postgres
spends real memory per connection. We put PgBouncer in front of the
database in transaction pooling mode, and connection count dropped from
about 4,000 to under 200. That alone bought us months of headroom.

[07:20] The second fix was read replicas. We added three streaming read
replicas and routed all dashboard and reporting queries to them, keeping
the primary for writes and transactional reads. Replication lag stays
under two seconds for us, which is fine for analytics.

[11:05] The third and biggest fix was declarative partitioning. We
partitioned the events table by month, so queries that filter on a time
range only scan the partitions they need, and dropping old data became a
metadata operation instead of a week-long DELETE. After partitioning,
p99 latency on the events table dropped from 450 milliseconds to about
80 milliseconds.

[15:30] A few lessons to take home. First, measure before you shard:
we never actually needed to shard, and I think most teams reach for
sharding too early. Second, boring tools like PgBouncer solve real
problems. Third, partitioning pays off only if your queries actually
filter on the partition key, so design that first.

[17:00] That's the story: pooling, replicas, and partitioning took us
from nightly pages to a system nobody thinks about. Thanks!
